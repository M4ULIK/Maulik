Project Overview

This project focused on developing a forward vehicle simulation framework to assess the energy and cost performance of heavy-duty battery-electric (BEV) and internal combustion engine (ICE) freight vehicles operating over real transport routes in Sweden. The work was conducted in collaboration with Volvo Group Trucks Technology (GTT) and ReVeRe to support ongoing research on freight electrification strategies.

Problem Statement

Electrification of heavy-duty freight transport presents challenges related to energy demand, route feasibility, and cost optimization. The goal was to simulate real-world operating conditions‚Äîincluding road topology, environmental effects, and driver behavior‚Äîto determine the energy cost differences and operational viability between ICE and BEV powertrains.

My Role & Responsibilities

  • Developed the Driver Model that adapts acceleration, braking, and speed control based on environmental factors such as road slope, curvature, and traffic density.
  • Implemented decision logic to reflect realistic driving strategies for both BEV and ICE powertrains within the forward simulation framework.
  • Collaborated with Volvo GTT and ReVeRe to validate environmental inputs and driver responses for alignment with real operational data.
  • Assisted in setting up and post-processing the MATLAB / Simulink framework, including environment and vehicle subsystems, to enable full route-based energy simulations.

Approach

  • Integrated the Driver subsystem into the simulation, ensuring compatibility with vehicle and environment models.
  • Designed the driver control algorithm to modulate throttle and brake inputs dynamically in response to topography and traffic inputs.
  • Supported the simulation of the Gothenburg‚ÄìViared freight route, using real gradient and weather profiles provided by project partners.
  • Post-processed results using MATLAB scripts to extract vehicle power demand, fuel / energy consumption, and operating cost across all simulated trips.
  • Iteratively refined control parameters based on performance feedback from professors and Volvo engineers.

Key Results & Outcomes

  • The battery-electric vehicle (BEV) demonstrated 25‚Äì45% higher energy efficiency and roughly 20% lower operational energy cost compared to the ICE configuration.
  • The model confirmed that a single charging station at Gothenburg Harbor was sufficient for daily freight operations under the simulated use cases.
  • The driver model successfully emulated realistic adaptive behavior, aligning with recorded operational data from Volvo.
  • Delivered a validated simulation platform for future studies in route optimization, driver strategy design, and freight energy forecasting.
