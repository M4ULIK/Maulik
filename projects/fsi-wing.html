FSI Analysis of a Formula Student Rear Wing

Conducted a Fluid-Structure Interaction (FSI) study on a Formula Student rear wing assembly to assess deformation, stress distribution, and safety under aerodynamic loads. The analysis integrated CFD and FEA simulations to validate the carbon fiber structure and ensure aerodynamic efficiency was maintained under load.

The goal was to understand how aerodynamic pressures affect composite deflection and identify necessary structural reinforcements.

Background

In Formula Student, rear wings generate substantial downforce but can experience structural deflection that alters aerodynamic performance. The analysis aimed to couple the aerodynamic loads from CFD with structural deformation analysis through a one-way FSI workflow.

The wing was designed from CFRP using a multi-element airfoil configuration, and the study ensured it could withstand event-specific load conditions without excessive deformation.

Objective

  • Quantify structural deformation of the rear wing under aerodynamic loading.
  • Validate the carbon fiber layup using FEA simulations.
  • Identify areas of stress concentration and possible weight reduction.
  • Ensure aerodynamic efficiency is not compromised by flexibility.

Methodology

  1. CFD simulation: Conducted steady-state aerodynamic analysis using STAR-CCM+ to obtain pressure distributions.
  2. Pressure transfer: Exported CFD pressure maps to ANSYS Workbench for structural analysis.
  3. FEA model: Created composite shell model of the rear wing in ANSYS Mechanical using material data from CFRP laminates.
  4. FSI coupling: Applied CFD pressures as surface loads to perform one-way FSI coupling.
  5. Validation: Compared deformation and stress levels to permissible composite limits.

The coupling allowed for a realistic representation of aerodynamic-structural interaction without the computational cost of full transient FSI.

Results & Insights

  • Maximum deformation observed at midspan of the main element; within safe design limits.
  • Composite layup provided adequate stiffness under operating loads.
  • Stress concentrations identified near endplates — improved by adding local reinforcement.
  • Verified the aerodynamic surface deflection did not significantly alter downforce.

The analysis improved understanding of real-world load behavior and helped refine future CFRP aero component designs.

Tools & Technologies

  • STAR-CCM+ – CFD simulation and pressure extraction.
  • ANSYS Workbench (Mechanical / ACP) – structural analysis and composite layup simulation.
  • CATIA V5 – CAD model of the rear wing geometry.
  • MATLAB / Excel – data processing and validation plots.
